\begin{block}{Research Objective}
  Rocket launch rates are rising, making sustainable recovery of payloads and first
  stages increasingly important. Autonomous surface vehicles (ASVs) can reduce cost,
  human exposure, and environmental impact, but require real-time prediction of
  the rocket landing location. Therefore, this work focuses on impact point prediction (IPP),
  which continuously estimates the landing location and uncertainty from onboard
  sensor data. The ASV uses this estimate for recovery guidance.

\end{block}

  \vspace{0.8em}
  \textbf{Navigation filter.} The Error-State Kalman Filter (ESKF) estimates position, velocity, attitude, and
  sensor biases from inertial propagation with GNSS, barometer, and magnetometer
  aiding. Its state and covariance initialise the IPP filter.

  \vspace{0.8em}
  \textbf{Impact point prediction filter.} The IPP filter is an Extended Kalman Filter
  (EKF). It propagates a Cartesian state
  $\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{p}^\top, \mathbf{v}^\top]^\top$ in NED coordinates forward
  until altitude reaches sea level:

  \vspace{0.2em}
  \begin{center}
  \begin{minipage}{0.97\columnwidth}
  \begin{equation*}
    \dot{\mathbf{p}} = \mathbf{v}, \qquad
    \dot{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{g}
      - K_D\,\|\mathbf{v}_a\|\,\mathbf{v}_a
      + \mathbf{R}(\hat{\mathbf{q}})\,\mathbf{a}_T, \qquad
    K_D = \frac{\rho\, C_D\, A}{2m}
  \end{equation*}
  \end{minipage}
  \end{center}

  \vspace{0.2em}
  The acceleration model includes gravity, drag, and thrust. The body-frame thrust
  $\mathbf{a}_T$ is rotated to NED by $\mathbf{R}(\hat{\mathbf{q}})$, where $\hat{\mathbf{q}}$
  is the ESKF attitude quaternion.

\end{block}

\begin{block}{Simulation Performance}
  The Cartesian IPP architecture was evaluated on four simulated trajectories using
  100 Monte Carlo wind realizations per trajectory. The rocket model was
  implemented with RocketPy, providing a controlled reference for evaluating
  the IPP under known model conditions.
  
  \vspace{0.5em}
  The IPP achieved 45.3\,m mean RMSE across the four trajectories. The plots show
  the median error and 5th--95th percentile spread.

\begin{block}{Discussion}
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.50\columnwidth}
    \vspace{0pt}
    \raggedright
    Simulation results show accurate IPP under controlled model conditions, but
    real flight exposes practical limits. The dominant issue was gyroscope
    saturation during burn, visible as the 40\,rad/s ceiling in the spin-rate
    plot.
  \end{minipage}\hfill
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.46\columnwidth}
    \vspace{0pt}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/poster_spin_rate.pdf}
  \end{minipage}

  \vspace{0.5em}
  This saturation is driven by the rocket's spin stabilization. Larger rockets
  commonly use active stabilization, avoiding this spin rate issue. Mounting the
  IMU closer to the spin axis
  or using a higher range gyroscope would reduce saturation. Beyond this
  hardware issue, better wind estimation, drag identification, parameter
  estimation, and uncertainty quantification would improve prediction accuracy
  and covariance bounds.
\end{block}
